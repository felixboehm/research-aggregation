# Biodegradable Polymers

## 1. Polylactic Acid (PLA)

### Production
PLA is produced by ring-opening polymerization of lactide, derived from fermentation of corn starch or sugarcane. It is the most commercially successful biodegradable polymer.

- Global production capacity: **~500,000 tonnes/year** (2025)
- Cost: $1.50–2.50/kg (vs. $1.00–1.50/kg for PET)
- Main producers: NatureWorks (USA), TotalEnergies Corbion (Netherlands)

### Degradation Pathway
PLA degrades via **hydrolysis** of ester bonds, followed by microbial assimilation:
1. Water penetrates the polymer matrix
2. Ester bonds cleave → lactic acid oligomers
3. Molecular weight drops below ~10,000 Da → material fragments
4. Microorganisms metabolize lactic acid → CO₂ + H₂O

**Critical requirement**: Industrial composting conditions (**58°C, >60% humidity**). PLA does NOT degrade meaningfully in:
- Home compost (too cold, ~35°C)
- Marine environment (too cold, wrong microbial community)
- Landfill (anaerobic, insufficient moisture)

### Degradation Timeline
| Environment | Time to Disintegration |
|---|---|
| Industrial composting (58°C) | 8–12 weeks |
| Soil burial (25°C) | 1–3 years |
| Marine water (15°C) | >10 years (effectively persistent) |

## 2. Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA)

### Production
PHA is produced by bacterial fermentation — microorganisms accumulate PHA granules as energy reserves under nutrient stress (nitrogen or phosphorus limitation).

- Cost: $3.00–6.00/kg (high due to fermentation yield)
- Main types: PHB (homopolymer, brittle), PHBV (copolymer, more flexible)
- Advantage: Biodegrades in **all environments** including marine and soil

### Degradation Pathway
PHA degrades via **enzymatic hydrolysis** by PHA depolymerases (widely present in soil and marine bacteria):
- No heat required — degrades at ambient temperature
- Marine degradation: **6–12 months** for thin films
- Soil degradation: **3–6 months**

### Limitations
- Brittle (PHB elongation at break: **3–5%** vs. ~400% for LDPE)
- Narrow processing window (degrades near melting point)
- Expensive compared to PLA and conventional plastics

## 3. Starch Blends

### Thermoplastic Starch (TPS)
- Starch + plasticizer (glycerol, sorbitol) → processable thermoplastic
- Extremely cheap raw material ($0.30–0.50/kg for corn starch)
- Biodegrades rapidly in all environments

### Limitations
- **Moisture-sensitive**: Absorbs water, loses mechanical properties
- Poor barrier properties (O₂ permeability ~100x higher than PET)
- Must be blended with PLA, PHA, or PBAT for practical use
